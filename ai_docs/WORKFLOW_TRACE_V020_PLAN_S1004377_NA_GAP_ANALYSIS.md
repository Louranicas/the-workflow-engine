## 3. Substrate-frame gaps — interrogating Plan v1

### NA-1 [SUBSTRATE] [HIGH] — DAW-1 "Tier 2 (wire-contracts) before Tier 1 (substrate primitives)" is a Frame-A geometry locked at draft-authorship; the substrate-frame defence is named as a tension but never produced

**The frame collapse.** § 1 of Plan v1 (L7) records the draft anchor decisions:

> *"DAW-1 Tier 2 (wire-contracts) lands before Tier 1 (substrate primitives) · DAW-2 full Plan v2 mirror"*

The Tier-2-first decision was made *before the gap analyses ran* and *before the Phase 4 interview surfaced anything*. The Frame-A defence (Plan v1 § 3 dependency graph L246-261, § 4 sequencing notes L264-267, § 10 T-2 L344-346) is consistent and reasonable: typed seams un-block Tier 3 cleanly; Tier 1 then consumes a typed surface. **Inside Frame A, this is correct.**

The substrate-frame question is whether the wire-contracts *encode a contract the substrate has not yet authored*. A `WorkflowProposal::escape_surface` field (W1, L57) is the engine asking the substrate "what shape will your refusal take?" *before* the substrate has had the chance to author its own refusal vocabulary via V1 RefusalToken. The engine-frame ordering ships the wire first and asks Tier 1 to make the substrate fit the wire. The substrate-frame ordering would ship V1 first (substrate-authored refusal vocabulary), let the engine **learn what the substrate's authorship surface looks like**, and let the wire-contract emerge from that learning — even if it means W1/W2 re-touch when V1 settles.

§ 10 T-2 (L344-346) names this tension and asserts: *"Luke chose Tier-2-first with eyes open. If the NA gap analyst returns 'this is Frame collapse', DX-W escalates to 'revisit DAW-1 first.'"* — i.e., the plan *delegates the substrate-frame defence to this document*. Plan v1 did not produce a substrate-frame argument *for* Tier-2-first; it produced an engineering argument and an escalation path.

**Second-order failure it hides.** If V1 RefusalToken lands at Phase 7 and its authorship variants force a W1 wire-contract regret-fix (e.g., `RefusalToken` needs an `EscapeSurfaceProfile`-typed payload field that W1 didn't account for), the regret-fix is a **second cross-binary serde wave** during Phase 7-11. The plan's risk register acknowledges this exactly once (L300, the second-last risk row), but classifies it as "Phase 4 reopens DAW-1 if NA surfaces evidence" — which makes this finding the *trigger event*, not a risk-register entry. If NA-1 is filed as MEDIUM, it does not trigger DAW-1 reopening. **Filing as HIGH is the substrate-frame action.**

**Recommendation (v2 fold):** Plan v2 must either (a) **revisit DAW-1 explicitly in Phase 4 Round A** (add `DX-DAW-1: Tier-1-first or Tier-2-first?` as a no-default question with both substrate and engineering framings produced *in the question itself*, not assumed in Tier-2-first's favour); **or** (b) **defend Tier-2-first from the substrate frame** by producing the argument the plan delegated to this pass — what does the substrate gain from receiving a typed seam before authoring its own refusal vocabulary? If neither defence holds, the rational v2 sequence is Phase 7 (V1) → Phase 5 (W1/W2/W3/W4) → Phase 6 (R1/R2/R3) → Phase 8-11 (V2-V5), making the wire-contract the **encoded learning** of V1, not the **slot V1 fills**. *Confidence: 0.85.*

### NA-3 [SUBSTRATE] [HIGH] — Round A "no defaults / load-bearing" + Round B "defaults acceptable" reproduces NA-3 from the v0.1.0 pass: the operator's *fatigue budget* is still the unspoken assumption shaping which questions get defaults

But the split's *boundary* — which questions are load-bearing — was made by the engine, not by asking the substrate (= Luke). Three of the eight Round B questions are substrate-shape decisions the operator might rate as load-bearing:

- **DX-2 (push or pull?)** — § 4 L144 / § 8 L181-185. The plan itself acknowledges (§ 9 L334) that *push* is the only honestly-Frame-B mode; *pull* is "more honestly Frame-A." Yet DX-2 is in Round B with a default-acceptable framing. The substrate-frame question is whether the engine emits or listens; that is structurally analogous to DX-V3 (own module vs distributed) which is in Round A. The classification difference is **arbitrary from the substrate frame**.
- **DX-5 (V4 fixture realism: full replicas or mock-only?)** — § 4 L147. The plan's own § 6 risk row L296 says: *"V4 fixture realism doesn't actually catch the production drifts it's designed for (Frame-A trap NA-1 redux)."* If the choice itself sets up a Frame-A trap, it is load-bearing. **Second-order failure it hides.** Plan v2's interview will run 11+ questions across Round A + Round B (§ 15 L420: "*Gap analyses will likely surface 8-16 more*"). With 19-27 questions and cheap defaults on most, the *predictable* outcome is that DX-2 = pull, DX-5 = mock-only, DX-1 = retain-4-variant are chosen by default — and v0.2.0 ships with the substrate-frame primitives degraded to their Frame-A floors. **Recommendation (v2 fold):** Promote DX-2, DX-5, and DX-1 to Round A (no defaults). Add an *operator-refusal* path per the v0.1.0 NA-3 recommendation: "this question is malformed / not now / present it again later" must be a first-class response, not silence routed to a default. Cap Round A at five questions per session (Luke's attention budget is the substrate); split additional load-bearing questions into Round A-2 with explicit fatigue acknowledgement. *Confidence: 0.85.*

### NA-4 [SUBSTRATE] [HIGH] — V3 m16 "substrate-drift canary" is engine-side aggregation flagged as such — but the V3 self-canary problem (§ 6 risk row L302) is "solved" by an answer that itself requires substrate participation v0.2.0 doesn't ship

**The frame collapse.** § 9 (L335) honestly labels V3 as engine-side aggregation: *"V3 is engine-side aggregation of substrate-observable state; the substrate-emitted version is post-v0.2.0."* That is genuine Frame-B labelling.

But § 6 risk row L302 says:

> *"Substrate-drift canary itself drifts (V3 self-canary problem) — V3 emits its own canary; m16 health pings stcortex / atuin / injection.db every cycle; if it fails to emit, the absence of canary events is itself a substrate-frame observable (m40 Nexus operator-visible alert)."*

Read this in the substrate frame: the engine claims that *the absence of an engine-emitted signal* is itself a substrate-observable. For absence-as-signal to function, **something else must be observing the absence on a different clock than the canary itself.** That something is m40 Nexus — but m40 Nexus is part of the same engine binary as m16. If m16 stops emitting because (a) the binary crashed, (b) the canary's own dependency chain (Tokio runtime, SQLite handle) died silently, or (c) a logic error suppressed the emit branch — m40 Nexus is *also affected* by the same root cause in ~all cases. The "absence as signal" mitigation is **the engine watching itself for absence**, which is exactly the self-canary problem one level deferred.

A true substrate-frame absence-detector requires a substrate-side observer with an independent clock — e.g., the Watcher's deployment-watch journal asserting `if missing(stcortex.workflow_trace_m16_heartbeat) for 2 cycles then emit alert`. Plan v1 does not propose this. § 11 (post-v0.2.0 partition L356-357) defers "m16 canary substrate-side emitter participation" — but does not defer the substrate-side *consumer* that would close the self-canary loop.

**Second-order failure it hides.** v0.2.0 ships V3 m16 with "self-canary covered" claimed in § 6 risk register. A production silent failure (the engine canary stops emitting, m40 Nexus is downstream of the same failure, no external observer is configured) is *exactly* the failure mode v0.1.0 had with diagnostics theatre (feedback memory `runbook_probe_freshness`). The v0.1.0 plan crystallised that pattern; v0.2.0 reproduces it inside the canary the v0.2.0 plan ships to detect it.

**Recommendation (v2 fold):** § 6 V3 self-canary mitigation must add: **Watcher's deployment-watch journal (or an equivalent substrate-side observer) asserts m16 heartbeat liveness; missing heartbeat for N cycles is a substrate-emitted alert, not engine-emitted.** If the Watcher is unavailable or the integration cannot ship in v0.2.0, the self-canary problem is **not mitigated** and the risk register must say so honestly. Adding "Watcher m16 heartbeat liveness assertion" to V5 SubstrateTrust as one of the substrates the engine queries closes the loop with primitives already in scope. *Confidence: 0.9.*

### NA-5 [SUBSTRATE] [HIGH] — V5 "in-engine receiver-only" is named as a first-class option but reading the fallback honestly: it ships an engine *imagining the substrate's voice* and routes the imagination through V1 RefusalToken `::Unavailable`

**The frame collapse.** § 11 Phase 11 (L216-220) and § 9 (L336) both honestly label the in-engine-receiver-only fallback for V5:

In Phase 11 step 2 (L217-219), when DX-V5 = receiver-only, "substrate side returns `Unavailable` per V1 RefusalToken pattern." Translation: the engine emits a `RefusalToken::Unavailable` *on the substrate's behalf*, attributes the refusal to the substrate, and proceeds. From outside the binary, this is indistinguishable from substrate-authored refusal. The substrate's *actual* voice (whether the stcortex consumer-trust schema exists, whether the Conductor dispatch-budget is queryable) is replaced by an engine-authored stand-in.

This is the **POVM write-only trap inverted**: instead of "engine writes without read-back-verify," it is "engine reads from a substrate it itself faked." The audit surface looks Frame-B (refusals are typed by authorship); the actual behaviour is engine-only.

§ 8 (L322-328) names the cross-habitat cut. But the named cut and the receiver-only fallback's `RefusalToken::Unavailable` collapse together in shipped behaviour: a sceptical observer of v0.2.0 ships will see typed refusals everywhere and **cannot distinguish substrate-authored from engine-self-authored**.

**Recommendation (v2 fold):** Add a discriminator to `RefusalToken::Unavailable`: a sub-tag `::Unavailable(EngineImagined | SubstrateUnreachable | SubstrateAuthored)`. *Engine-imagined* means the engine is fabricating the substrate's silence because the substrate has no participation primitive; this must be a **first-class telemetry event**, not a passive `None`. Add to V5 a `substrate_participation_status: enum { NotShipped, Shipping, Live }` accessor so that a future shift from EngineImagined to SubstrateAuthored is a substrate-frame transition the engine can announce. **Without this, in-engine receiver-only is Frame-A theatre wearing Frame-B vocabulary** — exactly what the discipline exists to prevent. *Confidence: 0.9.*

### NA-8 [SUBSTRATE] [MED] — V2 push/pull distinction (DX-2) treats substrate emitter participation as binary (ships or doesn't) when it is gradient (some substrates can emit; others won't for years)

**The frame collapse.** § 4 Round B (L144): *"DX-2 V2 substrate back-pressure signal shape — push (substrate emits to engine) or pull (engine probes substrate)?"* § 8 Phase 8 (L181-185) ships one mode for *all* substrates. § 11 (L357) defers push-mode substrate-side emitters as post-v0.2.0.

But the substrate landscape is heterogeneous. **stcortex** can ship a push-mode emitter (single-author repo, active mastery); **HABITAT-CONDUCTOR** likely can (workflow-trace-adjacent); **atuin** upstream very likely cannot in v0.2.0's timeframe (third-party). Choosing one mode globally forces the engine to either (a) over-engineer for the lowest-common-denominator substrate (pull mode globally) or (b) under-implement for the substrates that *can* push (push mode globally with `Unavailable` fallback for atuin, which silently degrades to pull-without-naming-it).
